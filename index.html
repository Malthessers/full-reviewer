Exploring Asia: The Largest Continent

Asia is the largest continent in the world by both land area and population. It covers an area of more than 44 million square kilometers, about 30% of Earth's total land area and 8% of Earth's total surface area.

East Asia

East Asian literature is the diverse writings from the East Asian nations, China, Japan, Korea, Mongolia and Taiwan. Literature from this area emerges as a distinct and unique field of prose and poetry that embodies the cultural, social and political factors of each nation.

China

China is one of the world's cradles of civilization, has started its unbroken literary tradition in the 14th century BCE. Poets like Du Fu, Li Po and Wang Wei of the Tang Dynasty has produced world-renowned literary works.

He wrote the poem "The Ballad of the Army of Cats" which is about conscription with hidden satire that speaks of the noticeable luxury of the court.

A Chinese poet who is a competitor of Du Fu as China's greatest poet.

ALONE AND DRINKING UNDER THE MOON

Amongst the flowers
I am alone with my pot of wine
Drinking by myself;
Then lifting my cup I asked the moon
To drink with me, it's reflection
And mine in the wine cup, just
The three of us; then I sigh
For the moon cannot drink,
And my shadow goes emptily along
With me never saying a word;
With no other friends here,
I can but use these two for company;
In the time of happiness,
I too must be happy with a;; Around me;
I sit and sing
And it is as if the moon accompanies me;
Then if I dance, it is my shadow that
Dances along with me:
While still not drunk, I am glad
To make the moon and my shadow
Into friends, but then when
I have drunk too much, we all part;
Yet these are friends I can always count on
These who have no emotion whatsoever;
I hope that one day we three will meet again,
Deep in the Milky Way.

Korea

Korea's literary tradition is greatly influenced by China's cultural dominance. As early as the 4th century CE, Korean poets wrote literary pieces in Classical Chinese poetry then transformations happened at the 7th century. Hangul, Korean's distinct writing system and national alphabet, is developed in the 15th century that gave new beginnings of Korean's literature.

In contemporary times, the Korean War has made a significant mark on Korean literature. In 1950, the themes present in the literary works are about alienation, conscience, disintegration, and self-identity.

CHOE NAM SEON

He was considered prominent historian, pioneering poet and publisher in the Korean literature.

He was also a leading member of the modern literary movement and became notable in pioneering modern Korean poetry.

One of his works, the poem "The Ocean to the Youth" made him a widely acclaimed poet.

The poem aimed to produce cultural reform. He sought to bring modern knowledge about the world to the youth of Korea.

YI KWANG SU

He was also the one who launched the modern literary movement together with Ch'oe Nam-Seon.

He was a novelist and wrote the first Korean novel "The Heartless" and became well-known because of it.

The Heartless: It was a description of the crossroads at which Korea found itself, stranded between tradition and modernity, and undergoing conflict between social realities and traditional ideals.

YUN HUNGGIL

He was a South Korean novelist who won the 1977 Korean Literature Writers Award.

He wrote the classic novel "Changma" (The Rainy Spell) that on a post-war family with two grandmothers and their shared grandson.

PAK KYONGNI

She was a South Korean poet and novelist.

She wrote the Korean's masterpiece and internationally acclaimed 21-volume epic novel Toji ("The Land"), wherein she chronicled the violent Korean history from 1897 to 1945.

European Literature

European literature includes some of the world's oldest literary traditions, from the epic poetry of ancient Greece, and Rome, to the modernist and post-modernist movements of the 20th century.

This period saw the birth of tragedy and comedy in Greek theater, with playwrights like Sophocles and Aristophanes, and the philosophical contributions of figures like Plato and Aristotle. Ancient Greek and Roman literature is characterized by its exploration of epic poetry, drama, and philosophy.

HOMER

The Iliad - An epic that focuses on the final weeks of the Trojan War, primarily highlighting the wrath of the Greek hero Achilles. The story ends with Hector's funeral rather than the fall of Troy.

The Odyssey - An epic that follows the Greek hero Odysseus on his long and challenging journey home after the Trojan War. It covers his adventures with mythical creatures, gods, and monsters as he struggles to return to Ithaca, where his wife and son await him.

VIRGIL

The Aeneid - A Latin epic poem that follows the journey of Aeneas, a Trojan hero after the fall of Troy. Aeneas is destined to found the city that will become Rome. The story begins with Aeneas fleeing Troy and undergoing various trials, including a love affair with the queen of Carthage, and encounters with mythical creatures, and different gods and goddesses.

Y Gododdin by Aneirin

This story commemorates the battle of the Catraeth around the sixth century. It tells the story of a band of warriors from the Kingdom of Gododdin.

Gododdin is now the modern-day Scotland who fought bravely against overwhelming AngloSaxon forces. Despite their courage and skill, almost all of the warriors perish in the battle.

The poem reflects themes of heroism, loyalty, and the tragic consequences of war, celebrating the fallen warriors while mourning their loss.

Beowulf

A old English epic poem that tells the story of its hero Beowulf, a warrior from Geatland.

Geatland is now the modern day Sweden. Beowulf travels to Denmark to help King Hrothgar. whose mead-hall is being terrorized by the monster named Grendel.

Beowulf defeats Grendel and later kills Grendel's mother who was seeking revenge. After returning home and becoming king, Beowulf faces his final challenge, defending his people from a dragon.

Though Beowulf slays the dragon, he is mortally wounded and later on dies, leaving behind a legacy of bravery and heroism.

The Renaissance 14th century â€“ 17th century

Saw an explosion of artistic and cultural activity emerging from years of plague, famine and political instability that marked the late Middle Ages.

Beginning in Italy in the 14th century and slowly spreading across Europe, numerous discoveries in science, astrology and geography brought sweeping changes to society.

A growing interest in humanist philosophy caused intellectuals to revisit the classical works of antiquity. The texts of the ancient Greeks and Romans, largely forgotten during the Middle Ages, began to inspire a new generation of European writers.

Around 1440, the German goldsmith Johannes Guttenberg invented the printing press, allowing literature to be reproduced and distributed on a previously unimaginable scale.

Prior to Guttenberg's invention, texts had to be painstakingly copied by hand at a rate ranging from 40 pages, to just, to just a few pages per workday, depending, of course, on the technique that is being used.

This tedious process understandably limited the availability of the literature. Guttenberg's press, however, was able to print up to 3,600 pages

Europe was soon producing millions of printed books, meaning that more authors were able to have their work published. Literacy rates and democratization of information begun.

Don Quixote

It follows the adventures of a man named Alonzo Quixano, who after reading too many chivalric romances. decides to become a knight-errant and adopts the name Don Quixote.

Accompanied by his loyal squire, Sancho Panza, he sets out on a quest to revive chivalry, battling windmills he believes to be giants and confusing everyday situations for grand adventures.

The novel is also a satirical exploration of idealism and reality, blending humor with deeper themes about dreams, madness, and the clash between fantasy.

Rise of Shakespeare

Drama was also a significant literary form during the Renaissance, particularly in Elizabethan England, where William Shakespeare (1564-1616) was busy establishing himself as one of the greatest English language writers of all time with plays that are still widely read and performed today such as Romeo and Juliet (1597) and Hamlet (1599-1601)

It is believed that Shakespeare wrote around 38 plays, including collaborations with other writers.

It follows the life of Jean Valjean, an ex-convict who is trying to redeem himself after serving 19 years in prison for stealing a loaf of bread.

Modernism Late 19th - Early 20th century

Towards the end of the 19th century, realism gave way to modernism. A literary movement that continued the realist tradition of social critique, but focused on the form and style of writing instead of plot and content.

This resulted in a variety of new narrative techniques including stream of consciousness narration, non-linear storylines, and multiple viewpoints.

Modernism was a literary movement of the late 19th and earlier 20th centuries.

Authors were inspired to break away from past literary conventions and play with their works form, style, and structure.

Writers like James Joyce, Franz Kafka, and Virginia Woolf set a penchant for experimentation that would carry on into the 21st century

Literary Techniques

Intertextuality is a literary convention where an author uses various techniques, including quotes, allusions, and more to refer to and connect their text to another.

Parody is an exaggerated imitation of a work or style for comedic effect and to mock or draw attention to specific elements of a text.

Metafiction is a literary technique where the author draws attention to the constructed nature of the text itself.

In European literature, many sub-movements among postmodern authors, such as the Theatre of the Absurd, was developed in reaction to the senselessness of World War II.

Theatre of the Absurd

Literary movement in the 1950s and 60s. Plays from this movement believed that life was essentially meaningless.

The Irish playwright Samuel Beckett is often cited as one of the most important figures in the theater of the absurd movement.

With his modern classic Waiting for Godot (1953) capturing the essential themes of existentialism and the meaningless of life.

ON THE ORIGIN OF EARTH AND MAN (BICOL MYTH)

Many, many years ago, there was no earth or man. There was only the sky. Now, in the sky there were two brothers, Bulan and Adlao. The latter was the older and the stronger. But the former was proud and hated his older brother.

One day they had a quarrel. Bulan hurled bad words at Adlao, claimed superiority, and challenged Adlao to a fight. The older brother only laughed at his younger brother. But his laugh was answered by Bulan who bellowed: "You coward, come and fight and I will show you my superiority. If you don't fight, I will kill you."

And Bulan suddenly rushed to Adlao without waiting for an answer. Adlao was angered and he was forced to fight his younger brother.

And the fight commenced. It was a clash between two strengths. With his dub, Bulan hit Adlao, but Adlao dodged the hit quickly. Then Bulan used his bolo, but again he missed Adlao. It was Adlao's turn to hit. So, with his club he hit with all his might, first the eye of Bulan, then the arm of Bulan which became flat at the might of the stroke. Then with his bolo he cut Bulan's flattened arm. When Bulan's eye was hit and his arm was flattened and cut from his body, he cried with pain. His tears fell on the cut flattened arm.

As Bulan foresaw his defeat with only one eye and one arm to fight with, he fled, and he was pursued by Adlao who was very angry and wanted to kill Bulan. And they kept running on and on, chasing each other.

Now, the cut flattened arm of Bulan with his tears fell. Down and down it went until it finally settled. The flattened arm of Bulan became the earth, and the tears became the rivers and seas. Time came when two hairs sprang from Bulan's cut arm, and from these two hairs sprang man and woman. Thus, the earth and man came into being.

Children of the City by Amadis Ma. Guerrero

Summary:

The story follows Victor, an eight-year-old boy whose father is a dock worker. His father gets involved in a labor strike against unfair wages and poor working conditions. The strike escalates into violence, and Victor's father is tragically killed. His mother, unable to cope with the loss, eventually abandons him, leaving him in the care of his uncle, Tio Pedring.

Forced to drop out of school, Victor is pushed into the harsh world of child labor as a newsboy. He navigates the dangers of Manila's streets, facing bullying, exploitation, and the reality of survival. He befriends Nacio, a lively and experienced newsboy, who teaches him the tricks of the trade. However, tragedy strikes again when Nacio is killed in a street accident.

As Victor continues his struggle, he gradually becomes hardened by the world around him. At first, he resists the rough behavior of other newsboys, refusing to curse, smoke, or engage in their reckless habits. However, after a violent altercation, he finally gives inâ€”accepting a cigarette as a symbol of his complete transformation

The story depicts the loss of innocence, showing how Victor, once a hopeful child, is shaped by the harsh realities of the city. It critiques social injustices, child labor, and the cycle of poverty that forces children into lives of hardship and crime.

Footnote to Youth by Jose Garcia Villa

The story follows Dodong, a 17-year-old boy who believes he is ready for adulthood. He decides to marry Teang despite his father's hesitation. Dodong is confident in his choice, feeling that marriage will bring happiness and fulfillment.

However, soon after marriage, reality sets in. Teang becomes pregnant, and Dodong realizes the burden of responsibility that comes with having a family. As the years pass, more children arrive, leaving Teang exhausted and regretful. She wonders if her life would have been different had she married Lucio, an older suitor who remained childless.

Dodong, too, begins to question his youthful decision. One night, he steps outside, seeking answers about why life does not fulfill youth's dreams. He realizes that youth is blinded by love and desire, but real life is far more difficult than expected.

Years later, Dodong's son, Blas, comes home excited to tell him that he also wishes to marry young. Dodong, now older and wiser, does not want his son to go through the same struggles. However, he feels powerless, knowing that youth will always choose love first and learn the hardships laterâ€”just as he did.

The story reflects on the cycle of life and how every generation must learn from its own experiences, rather than from the warnings of others.

How My Brother Leon Brought Home a Wife by Manuel E. Arguilla

Baldo narrates the journey of his brother Leon (Noel) as he brings his city-bred wife, Maria, home to their rural hometown of Nagrebcan, La Union from Manila. Instead of taking the usual route through the Camino Real, Baldo follows their father's instructions and takes them through the Waig, a rough, dry riverbed, as a test of Maria's adaptability to provincial life.

Riding on a cart pulled by Labang, their family's bull, Baldo observes Maria's grace and poise despite the unfamiliar, rugged path. She remains cheerful, admiring the countryside and showing deep affection for Leon, calling him "Noel." Her warm demeanor reassures Baldo that she truly loves his brother and is willing to embrace rural life.

Upon arriving home, their father quietly evaluates Maria through Baldo's account of the journey. Though he does not explicitly state his approval, Maria's patience and respect suggest that she has passed the test. The story highlights love, acceptance, and the contrast between urban and rural life, with Labang symbolizing the steady, unchanging nature of the countryside. The Taximan by Catherine Lim

The Taximan is a short story by Catherine Lim that provides insight into Singaporean society through the perspective of a chatty and opinionated taxi driver. The story is narrated by the taximan himself as he shares his views on modern youth, morality, and changing cultural values.

The taximan recounts his experiences driving young customers, particularly teenage schoolgirls, around the city. He expresses frustration with the new generation, criticizing their lack of discipline, obsession with money, and preference for Westernized lifestyles. He frequently complains about how these girls lie to their parents, skip school, and visit hotels with older men for money.

Despite his disapproval, the taximan admits that he profits from this situation. He earns extra income by driving these girls to their secret destinations, even though he considers their actions immoral. His hypocrisy is revealed when he condemns the behavior but continues to benefit financially from it.

Towards the end of the story, his disapproval turns personal when he shares that his own daughter has been caught engaging in similar behavior. This deeply upsets him, exposing his double standardsâ€”while he condemns other girls, he is devastated when the same thing happens in his own family.

Key Points

  1. Generational Conflict: The taximan criticizes modern youth, seeing them as morally corrupt compared to his own generation.
  2. Hypocrisy: Although he looks down on these young girls, he benefits from their behavior by driving them around for extra money.
  3. Cultural and Moral Decline: He laments the loss of traditional values in Singaporean society, blaming Western influences.
  4. Irony: His own daughter ends up engaging in the very behavior he despises, making his frustration deeply personal.
  5. Satire and Social Commentary: The story highlights hypocrisy in moral judgment and critiques how people often condemn others while acting in self-interest.
